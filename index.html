Astrobiology Integrative Research Framework

For astrobiology to be truly interdisciplinary, an integrative mechanism is required to reveal how its diverse constituent subject areas are related.

To that end, as part of the NASA Astrobiology Institute team at the University of Hawaii, we're developing AIRFrame, an integrative knowledge framework based on open XML standards, that will:

  • Relate diverse research both conceptually (by subject, etc.) and functionally (by researcher, mission, research questions, NASA/NAI goals, etc.)

  • Allow Web-browsable concept-map type visualizations of these relationships for researchers, students, and the public.

  • Encourage collaboration by allowing researchers to identify those in other fields who are working on similar questions.

  • Create a persistent framework for the interdisciplinary education of future astrobiology researchers.

  • Support the NASA Astrobiology Institute Collaborative Science Plan
We envision AIRFrame as a tool where astrobiology researchers, students, and the public can generate and view non-obvious linkages between diverse constituent areas. In this way, AIRFrame can support and inform interdisciplinary astrobiology research and give the next generation of astrobiology researchers a more interdisciplinary perspective about the questions of life in the universe.

This research is supported by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration through the NASA Astrobiology Institute under Cooperative Agreement No. NNA08DA77A issued through the Office of Space Science.


Current projects

  • AIRFrame technical infrastructure and visualization software evaluation
  • Quantification of the disciplinary roots of astrobiology
  • Ontology creation for astrobiological terms, concepts and relations
  • Exploration of automatic document classification feasibility
  • Review of prior work on computer supported scientific collaboratories

Workflow

AIRFrame does not rely on direct researcher input. Our research team is collecting and analysing astrobiology content and integrative works, such as:

  • Published scientific literature
  • The NAI Library collection
  • The NASA Astrobiology Roadmap
  • NAI reports and focus group proceedings
  • AbSciCon and other astrobiology conference proceedings

to ground the creation of the framework relationships.
